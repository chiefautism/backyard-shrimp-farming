## Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Native range | Taiwan, China, Korea |
| Max size | 2.5-3 cm (females larger) |
| Lifespan | 1-2 years |
| Temperature | 18-28°C (65-82°F) |
| pH | 6.5-8.0 |
| Breeding | Extremely easy - no special conditions |
| Diet | Biofilm, algae, detritus, supplemental food |
| Difficulty | Beginner |

Neocaridina davidi is the most popular freshwater ornamental shrimp in the world. They come in a rainbow of color morphs, breed readily in aquariums, and can generate legitimate income from a spare bedroom.

## Water Parameters

| Parameter | Optimal | Acceptable | Danger |
|-----------|---------|------------|--------|
| Temperature | 20-26°C (68-79°F) | 18-28°C | >30°C, <15°C |
| pH | 6.8-7.5 | 6.5-8.0 | <6.0, >8.5 |
| GH | 6-10 dGH | 4-14 dGH | <3, >18 |
| KH | 2-6 dKH | 1-8 dKH | 0 (pH instability) |
| TDS | 180-280 | 150-350 | >400 |
| Ammonia | 0 | 0 | ANY detectable amount |
| Nitrite | 0 | 0 | ANY detectable amount |
| Nitrate | <10 mg/L | <20 mg/L | >40 mg/L |
| Copper | 0 | 0 | **LETHAL** even in traces |

**Copper warning**: Many fish medications, algaecides, and even some fertilizers contain copper. Copper kills shrimp. Check EVERYTHING you put in the tank.

**Stability > perfection**. Neocaridina tolerate a wide range but hate sudden changes. A stable pH of 7.8 is better than pH swinging between 6.8 and 7.2.

## Tank Setup

### The Basics

| Item | Recommendation | Cost |
|------|---------------|------|
| Tank | 10-20 gallon (per breeding colony) | $15-50 (Petco $/gal sale) |
| Filter | Sponge filter (dual sponge preferred) | $8-15 |
| Air pump | Small, quiet | $8-15 |
| Heater | Optional (they're fine at room temp in most homes) | $15-25 |
| Light | Low-medium, 6-8 hours/day | $15-40 |
| Substrate | Inert gravel, sand, or active substrate | $10-25 |
| Plants | Java moss (mandatory), java fern, anubias, floating plants | $10-30 |
| Hardscape | Cholla wood, driftwood, rocks | $5-15 |

**Total startup per colony: $80-200**

### Why Sponge Filter is Mandatory

- HOB and canister filters suck up baby shrimp
- Sponge filter provides biological filtration + surface area for biofilm (shrimp food!)
- Gentle flow doesn't stress shrimp
- Cheap and reliable
- If you must use another filter type, put a sponge pre-filter over the intake

### Plants That Work Best

| Plant | Why |
|-------|-----|
| **Java Moss** | #1 shrimp plant. Provides hiding spots for babies, surface for biofilm. Get lots of it. |
| **Subwassertang** | Similar benefits to java moss, slightly different look |
| **Java Fern** | Hardy, low-light, provides cover |
| **Anubias** | Hardy, low-light, broad leaves collect biofilm |
| **Floating plants** (Salvinia, Frogbit, Water Lettuce) | Provides shade, absorbs nitrates, roots are baby shelter |
| **Marimo Moss Ball** | Biofilm surface, aesthetic |
| **Bucephalandra** | Hardy, beautiful, good biofilm surface |

**Do NOT use CO2 injection** unless you really know what you're doing. CO2 swings pH, which kills shrimp.

### Other Important Items

- **Indian Almond Leaves (Catappa)**: Release tannins, mildly antibacterial, shrimp love to graze on decomposing leaves. Add 1-2 per 10 gallons.
- **Cholla Wood**: Cactus skeleton. Biofilm magnet, hiding spots.
- **Mineral Rocks/Montmorillonite Clay**: Provides trace minerals for molting.

## Breeding

### The Easy Part

Neocaridina breed readily with zero intervention. If you have males and females in appropriate water, they WILL breed.

  <br/><sub><i>Berried female carrying eggs under her tail. Photo: Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA</i></sub>
</p>

### Sexing

| Feature | Male | Female |
|---------|------|--------|
| Size | Smaller (1.5-2 cm) | Larger (2-3 cm) |
| Color | Often paler | Deeper color |
| Saddle | No saddle | Yellow/green saddle behind head (eggs in ovaries) |
| Underbelly | Flat/straight | Curved (for carrying eggs) |
| Behavior | More active swimmers | More sedentary grazers |

### The Breeding Process

![Neocaridina Breeding Cycle](../../diagrams/breeding-cycle-neocaridina.svg)

1. Female develops **saddle** (eggs visible behind head through shell)
2. Female molts -> releases pheromones
3. Males go crazy swimming around (the "mating dance")
4. Male fertilizes eggs
5. Female moves eggs to **pleopods** (swimmerets under tail) - now she's "berried"
6. Female carries eggs for **28-35 days**, fanning them constantly
7. Fully developed shrimplets hatch as **miniature adults** (no larval stage!)
8. Shrimplets are 1-2mm, immediately independent, eat biofilm

### Maximizing Breeding Rate

- Colony of 10+ (3:1 female to male ratio ideal)
- Stable parameters (no sudden changes)
- Temperature 24-26°C (warmer = faster breeding)
- Lots of biofilm (BacterAE helps)
- Abundant hiding spots (java moss!)
- Don't overclean the tank (biofilm is food)
- Multiple females berried simultaneously in a healthy colony

## Color Genetics & Compatibility

**THIS IS CRITICAL. Mixing wrong color lines produces ugly wild-type offspring.**

### The Rule

Colors derived from the **same base line** will interbreed and may produce wild-type (brown) offspring. Keep separate.

![Color Compatibility Chart](../../diagrams/neocaridina-color-chart.svg)

### Compatibility Chart

| Line Group | Colors | Can Mix? |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Red Line** | Cherry, Fire Red, Painted Fire Red, Sakura | Yes (same line) |
| **Blue Line** | Blue Dream, Blue Velvet | Yes (same line) |
| **Orange Line** | Orange Sakura, Sunkist | Yes (same line) |
| **Yellow Line** | Yellow, Golden Back, Neon Yellow | Yes (same line) |
| **Chocolate Line** | Chocolate, Black Rose | Yes (same line) |
| **Wild** | Wild type, Rili variants | Yes (same base) |
| **Bloody Mary** | Bloody Mary only | Separate (tissue color, not shell) |

### Mixing Between Groups

**Red + Blue = Wild Type offspring** (brown/clear)
**Red + Yellow = Wild Type offspring**
**Any different line groups = revert to Wild Type over generations**

**Rule**: One color per tank. Period. Unless you want a tank of brown shrimp.

### Culling

To maintain and improve color quality:
1. Separate low-grade shrimp from colony (every 2-4 weeks)
2. Move culls to a "cull tank" or sell as lower grade
3. Only keep the best-colored specimens as breeders
4. Selectively breed darkest/most opaque individuals
5. Introduce new bloodlines every 6-12 months to prevent inbreeding

## Feeding

### Primary Diet: Biofilm

70%+ of a Neocaridina's diet in a well-established tank is **biofilm** - the invisible layer of bacteria, algae, and microorganisms on every surface. A mature tank with lots of surfaces (sponge filter, java moss, wood, leaves) feeds shrimp naturally.

### Supplemental Feeding

| Food | Frequency | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **BacterAE** | Every other day (tiny pinch) | Boosts biofilm growth - this is the #1 supplement |
| **Shrimp King Complete** | 2-3x/week | Good all-around pellet |
| **Blanched Vegetables** | 1-2x/week | Spinach, zucchini, cucumber (remove after 6 hours) |
| **Snowflake Food** | 1-2x/week | Soybean husks, doesn't foul water |
| **Indian Almond Leaves** | Always have 1-2 in tank | They graze on decomposing leaf |
| **Mulberry Leaves** | Replace as consumed | Natural food, great for color |
| **Mineral/Calcium Food** | 1x/week | Shrimp King Mineral, cuttlebone, etc. |
| **Protein** (frozen bloodworm, etc.) | 1x/week (small amount) | Helps with breeding, don't overdo it |

### Feeding Rules

1. **Less is more** - a biofilm-rich tank barely needs supplemental food
2. Remove uneaten food after 2-6 hours
3. If shrimp ignore food, you're overfeeding
4. A colony of 50 shrimp needs a piece of food the size of a pea
5. Fasting 1-2 days per week is fine and even beneficial

## Common Problems

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Deaths after water change | Temperature or parameter shock | Match temp within 1°C, drip acclimate new water, change max 20% at a time |
| Failed molts | Low GH/minerals, sudden parameter change | Maintain GH 6-10, use remineralizer, add cuttlebone/mineral food |
| Color loss over generations | Not culling, inbreeding | Cull low-grade regularly, introduce new bloodlines |
| Not breeding | Immature, wrong sex ratio, stress | Wait for maturity (3-4 months), check parameters, ensure food/biofilm |
| Planaria | Overfeeding, introduced on plants | Reduce feeding, Planaria Zero (fenbendazole), manual trapping |
| Hydra | Introduced on plants | Fenbendazole treatment, manual removal |
| Scutariella japonica | Parasite (worm on head) | Salt dip (1 tbsp/L for 30-60 seconds), repeat in 1 week |
| Vorticella | Protozoan on shell | Salt dip, improve water quality |
| Green algae explosion | Too much light, excess nutrients | Reduce light hours, reduce feeding, add floating plants |
| Shrimp not eating | Stress, bad water quality | Test parameters, do partial water change |
| Baby shrimp disappearing | Filter intake, fish predators, poor food | Use sponge filter only, no fish in shrimp tank, ensure biofilm |

### The #1 Rule for Neocaridina

**Stability over perfection.** Don't chase perfect numbers. Find stable parameters in your water supply and let them be. Neocaridina adapt. Chasing pH and constantly adjusting kills more shrimp than "imperfect" but stable water ever will.
