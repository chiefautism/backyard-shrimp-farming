# Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS)

> Filtered, controlled, precise. More equipment than biofloc, but clearer water and finer control.

## Overview

A RAS circulates water through external filtration components that remove waste and maintain water quality. Unlike biofloc (where the water IS the filter), RAS separates the grow tank from the filtration.

### RAS vs Biofloc

| Factor | RAS | Biofloc |
|--------|-----|---------|
| Water clarity | Clear | Brown/turbid |
| Equipment cost | Higher | Lower |
| Complexity | More components | Simpler setup |
| Water exchange | 10-20% weekly | Near-zero |
| Stocking density | Lower (30-50/m³) | Higher (50-300/m³) |
| Management | Equipment maintenance | Water chemistry |
| Failure mode | Equipment failure | Floc crash, alkalinity crash |
| Best for | Ornamental shrimp, precise control | Vannamei production |

## System Components

### 1. Grow Tank
Same options as biofloc (IBC totes, stock tanks, aquariums). Round preferred for water flow.

### 2. Mechanical Filtration (Solids Removal)
Removes physical waste particles before they decompose.

| Type | How It Works | Cost | Maintenance |
|------|-------------|------|-------------|
| Settling tank/clarifier | Gravity settles heavy particles | $20-50 (DIY) | Drain sludge weekly |
| Radial flow separator | Water spirals, solids settle to center | $30-80 (DIY) | Drain sludge weekly |
| Filter sock/pad | Physical barrier catches particles | $5-15 | Clean/replace weekly |
| Drum filter | Rotating screen, self-cleaning | $500+ | Minimal (automated) |

For backyard scale: **A simple settling tank or radial flow separator works fine.**

### 3. Biological Filtration
Bacteria convert ammonia -> nitrite -> nitrate. The core of any RAS.

| Type | Media | Cost | Capacity | Notes |
|------|-------|------|----------|-------|
| Moving Bed Biofilter (MBBR) | K1/K3 media | $30-80 | High | Best for backyard RAS. Aerated container with floating media. |
| Fluidized Bed | Sand/small media | $40-100 | Very high | Compact, efficient, needs pump |
| Trickle Filter | Bio-balls, lava rock | $30-60 | Medium | Exposed to air, good O2 |
| Sponge Filter | Sponge | $8-15 | Low-Medium | Fine for ornamental shrimp tanks |
| Hang-on-Back (HOB) | Cartridge/media | $20-40 | Low-Medium | Convenient for small tanks |

**For food shrimp**: MBBR or fluidized bed.
**For ornamental shrimp**: Sponge filter (with pre-filter on intake to protect babies).

### 4. Protein Skimmer (Saltwater Only)
Removes dissolved organics before they break down.

| Size | Rating | Cost | Notes |
|------|--------|------|-------|
| Small (up to 100 gal) | Budget | $30-80 | Adequate for single tank |
| Medium (100-300 gal) | Mid-range | $80-200 | Better for multiple tanks |

Optional but recommended for saltwater vannamei RAS.

### 5. UV Sterilizer (Optional)
Kills free-floating pathogens and algae.

| Size | Flow Rate | Cost | Notes |
|------|-----------|------|-------|
| 9-13W | 50-100 GPH | $30-60 | Single tank |
| 24-36W | 200-400 GPH | $60-120 | Multiple tanks |

Good insurance against disease. Not strictly necessary but nice to have.

### 6. Sump/Reservoir
Central water collection point. Can be a simple Rubbermaid tub.
- Houses pump, heater, probe sensors
- Allows for easy water level management
- Auto top-off can be added

### 7. Pump
Circulate water through the system.

**Target turnover**: 2-4x total system volume per hour.
- 300 gallon system = 600-1200 GPH pump
- Submersible or external

## DIY RAS Build

### Budget Version (~$200-500)

For a single 50-100 gallon tank:

**Components**:
1. Grow tank (stock tank or aquarium): $50-150
2. 5-gallon bucket settling tank: $5
3. 5-gallon bucket MBBR (with K1 media + air stone): $30-50
4. Small submersible pump (200-400 GPH): $15-25
5. PVC plumbing (3/4" pipe, fittings): $20-30
6. Air pump for MBBR aeration: $15-25
7. Heater: $20-40
8. Overflow/standpipe: $10
9. **Total: $165-335**

**Flow path**: Grow tank -> overflow -> settling bucket -> MBBR bucket -> pump -> back to grow tank

### Mid-Range Version (~$500-1500)

For 2-4 tanks (200-800 gallons total):

Add to budget version:
- Larger sump (30-50 gal Rubbermaid): $30-50
- Better pump (800-1200 GPH): $40-80
- Protein skimmer (if saltwater): $60-150
- UV sterilizer: $40-80
- More K1 media: $20-40
- Better plumbing (bulkheads, ball valves): $40-60
- Auto top-off system: $30-50

## Clear Water Setup (Ornamental Shrimp)

For Neocaridina and Caridina, a full RAS is overkill. The "clear water" approach is simpler:

### Components
1. **Tank**: 10-20 gallon aquarium
2. **Sponge filter**: Dual sponge, air-driven
3. **Air pump**: Small, quiet
4. **Substrate**: Inert gravel/sand (Neocaridina) or buffering substrate (Caridina)
5. **Plants**: Java moss, java fern, anubias, floating plants
6. **Heater**: Optional for most rooms
7. **Light**: Low-medium, timer-controlled

### Why This Works
- Low bioload (small shrimp, low stocking)
- Plants absorb nitrate
- Sponge filter provides biological filtration
- Weekly 10-20% water changes handle the rest
- Simple, reliable, cheap

## Water Management

### Exchange Schedule

| System | Exchange Amount | Frequency |
|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Full RAS (food shrimp) | 10-20% | Weekly |
| Full RAS (ornamental) | 10-15% | Weekly |
| Clear water (ornamental) | 10-20% | Weekly |

### Biofilter Maintenance

- **MBBR**: Self-maintaining (media tumbles, old biofilm sloughs off). Just keep aeration running.
- **Trickle filter**: Rinse media in OLD tank water every 1-2 months. Never use tap water (kills bacteria).
- **Sponge filter**: Squeeze in old tank water every 2-4 weeks.

**NEVER clean all filtration at once.** Clean one component at a time to preserve bacteria.

## When to Choose RAS Over Biofloc

Choose RAS if:
- You want clear water (ornamental shrimp, display tanks)
- You prefer equipment management over water chemistry management
- You're raising ornamental shrimp (Neocaridina, Caridina)
- You want finer control over individual parameters
- Your setup is indoors and aesthetics matter

Choose biofloc if:
- You want maximum shrimp per volume
- You're raising vannamei for food
- You want lower equipment cost
- You're comfortable with brown/turbid water
- You want near-zero water exchange
