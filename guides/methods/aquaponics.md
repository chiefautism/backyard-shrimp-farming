# Aquaponics Integration

> Shrimp feed plants. Plants clean water. You harvest both. The dual-income approach.

## Overview

Aquaponics combines aquaculture (raising aquatic animals) with hydroponics (growing plants in water). Shrimp waste provides nutrients for plants, and plants filter the water for shrimp.

![Aquaponics System](../../diagrams/aquaponics-system.svg)

### Why Add Plants?

- **Dual harvest**: Shrimp + vegetables/herbs
- **Natural filtration**: Plants absorb nitrate, phosphate, and trace nutrients
- **Reduced water changes**: Plants handle nutrient removal
- **Extra income**: Fresh herbs and lettuce sell well at farmers markets
- **Looks impressive**: Full ecosystem in your backyard

### The Challenge

Shrimp and plants have different optimal pH ranges. This is THE main issue.

| Organism | Optimal pH |
|----------|-----------|
| L. vannamei | 7.5-8.5 |
| M. rosenbergii | 7.0-8.5 |
| Most vegetables | 5.5-6.5 |
| Compromise zone | 6.8-7.2 |

You'll operate at a pH compromise (~7.0) that isn't perfect for either but workable for both.

## System Types

### Media Bed

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| How it works | Water floods and drains through gravel/clay pebble beds |
| Media | Expanded clay (Hydroton), lava rock, gravel |
| Pros | Simple, provides mechanical + biological filtration, flood/drain action aerates roots |
| Cons | Heavy, can clog, limited plant types |
| Best plants | Leafy greens, herbs, strawberries, peppers |
| Cost | $50-150 per bed |

### Deep Water Culture (DWC/Raft)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| How it works | Plants float on styrofoam rafts, roots dangle in nutrient water |
| Pros | High yield, easy harvest, scalable |
| Cons | Needs separate solids filtration, roots can clog |
| Best plants | Lettuce, basil, bok choy, watercress |
| Cost | $30-80 per raft |

### Nutrient Film Technique (NFT)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| How it works | Thin film of water flows through channels, roots absorb nutrients |
| Pros | Lightweight, space-efficient, vertical farming possible |
| Cons | Vulnerable to pump failure, limited to smaller plants |
| Best plants | Herbs, lettuce, strawberries |
| Cost | $40-100 per channel set |

**Best for shrimp aquaponics**: Media bed (simplest integration) or DWC (highest yield).

## Shrimp Species for Aquaponics

### Best: M. rosenbergii (Freshwater Prawn)
- True freshwater = full plant compatibility
- Larger animals = more waste = more plant nutrients
- Slower growth balances with plant growth cycles

### Workable: L. vannamei (at low salinity)
- Must adapt to low salinity (2-5 ppt) or freshwater
- Limits plant options at higher salinity
- Faster growth cycle

### Small-scale: Ornamental Shrimp
- Cherry shrimp + small herb/lettuce system
- Low bioload = small plant bed needed
- More aesthetic/educational than productive

## Basic Setup Guide

### Small System (Beginner, ~$200-400)

**Shrimp tank**: 100-gallon stock tank or 2x IBC totes
**Plant bed**: 2x 4ft media beds (expanded clay) or 1 DWC raft (4x4 ft)
**Pump**: 200-400 GPH submersible
**Plumbing**: 3/4" PVC, bell siphon (media bed) or constant flow (DWC)
**Timer**: Flood and drain timer for media beds

**Flow**: Shrimp tank -> pump -> plant beds -> gravity return to shrimp tank

### Key Ratios

| Ratio | Guideline |
|-------|-----------|
| Feed rate to plant area | 60-100g feed/day per m² of plant bed |
| Shrimp tank to plant bed | 1:1 to 1:2 volume ratio |
| Fish/shrimp to plants | 1 lb of shrimp supports ~7 plants (roughly) |
| Stocking to filtration | Start light, add more shrimp as plants establish |

## Balancing the System

### Nutrient Cycle

```
Shrimp eat feed
    ↓
Shrimp produce ammonia (waste)
    ↓
Bacteria convert ammonia → nitrite → nitrate (in media bed or biofilter)
    ↓
Plants absorb nitrate + phosphate + micronutrients
    ↓
Clean water returns to shrimp tank
```

### Common Deficiencies

Plants in aquaponics often lack:
- **Iron**: Most common deficiency. Supplement with chelated iron (Fe-DTPA at pH <7, Fe-EDDHA at pH >7)
- **Potassium**: Add potassium hydroxide or potassium carbonate (also raises pH/alkalinity)
- **Calcium**: Add calcium carbonate (also raises alkalinity)

### pH Management

This is the eternal struggle in shrimp aquaponics.

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| pH too high for plants | Use slightly acidic media, increase plant mass |
| pH too low for shrimp | Add calcium/potassium carbonate, reduce organic loading |
| pH swinging | Increase alkalinity buffer, add more system volume |

**Target pH 7.0** - compromise that both shrimp and plants can tolerate.

## Common Challenges

| Challenge | Cause | Solution |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Plants yellowing | Iron or potassium deficiency | Supplement chelated iron, add K source |
| Nitrate too high (plants can't keep up) | Too much feed, not enough plants | Add more plant capacity, reduce feeding |
| Root rot | Low oxygen at roots, pathogens | Ensure good flow/aeration at root zone |
| Algae in plant beds | Too much light on water surface | Shade water surfaces, increase plant coverage |
| Pest insects on plants | Outdoor system, natural occurrence | Manual removal, neem oil (CAREFUL - toxic to shrimp at high concentrations) |
| Shrimp dying, plants fine | Water quality issue in shrimp tank | Test ammonia/nitrite/DO at shrimp tank |
| Plants growing slowly | System not mature, low nutrients | Be patient, takes 1-3 months to balance |

### CRITICAL: No Pesticides!

**NEVER use commercial pesticides, herbicides, or most fungicides in a shrimp aquaponics system.** They will kill your shrimp. Even "organic" products can be lethal.

Safe pest control:
- Manual removal
- Companion planting (basil deters some pests)
- Diatomaceous earth (on dry surfaces only, not in water)
- Introduce beneficial insects (ladybugs, lacewings)
