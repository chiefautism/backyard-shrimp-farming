## Federal Requirements

Three primary federal agencies regulate aspects of shrimp farming. Even small backyard operations can fall under their jurisdiction depending on scale, species, and how product is sold.

### USDA / APHIS (Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service)

| Area | Details |
|---|---|
| **Jurisdiction** | Interstate movement of live aquatic animals, disease control |
| **Key regulation** | Title 9 CFR, Parts 71-75 (animal health); Lacey Act (illegal wildlife trade) |
| **When it applies** | Importing live shrimp (especially non-native species), shipping live animals across state lines |
| **Permits needed** | APHIS import permit (VS 16-3) for importing live crustaceans from abroad; health certificates for interstate transport of live animals in many cases |
| **Disease concerns** | APHIS monitors for OIE-listed diseases (WSSV, TSV, EMS/AHPND, YHV). Outbreaks may trigger quarantine orders |
| **Practical note** | If you buy SPF postlarvae from a domestic hatchery and farm within one state, APHIS involvement is minimal. Importing broodstock or PL from overseas triggers the full permitting process |

### EPA (Environmental Protection Agency)

| Area | Details |
|---|---|
| **Jurisdiction** | Water discharge, pesticide use, wetlands |
| **Key regulation** | Clean Water Act (CWA); NPDES permit program |
| **When it applies** | Discharging water from ponds, tanks, or raceways into waters of the US (streams, ditches connected to waterways, storm drains) |
| **Threshold** | Concentrated Aquatic Animal Production (CAAP) facility rules apply at >100,000 lbs annual harvest for cold water or >100,000 lbs for warm water species. Most backyard farms are well below this, but state-level thresholds may be lower |
| **Practical note** | Even small operations should not discharge nutrient-rich water directly into natural waterways. Using discharge water for garden irrigation is a common compliant approach |

### FDA (Food and Drug Administration)

| Area | Details |
|---|---|
| **Jurisdiction** | Food safety for shrimp sold as food (fresh, frozen, processed) |
| **Key regulation** | 21 CFR Part 123 (Seafood HACCP); Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) |
| **When it applies** | Any sale of shrimp for human consumption |
| **Requirements** | HACCP plan, facility registration with FDA, proper labeling, traceability records |
| **Exemptions** | Direct farm-to-consumer sales at farmers markets may qualify for reduced requirements under state cottage food or farm-direct sales laws, but seafood is often excluded from cottage food exemptions due to safety concerns |
| **Practical note** | Even small-scale sellers typically need a HACCP plan. Many states offer HACCP training through Sea Grant or extension programs |

### Other Federal Considerations

- **US Army Corps of Engineers** -- Required permits (Section 404) if you plan to construct ponds in wetland areas or alter waterways.
- **US Fish & Wildlife Service** -- May be involved if your operation could affect endangered species or if you farm non-native species in open systems.
- **SBA / IRS** -- Standard business registration, EIN, and tax obligations apply when selling commercially.

## State-by-State Overview

Requirements vary significantly. The table below covers key shrimp-farming states. Contact your state's department of agriculture and natural resources for current details.

| State | Aquaculture Permit/License | Approx. Cost | Species Restrictions | Key Agency | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Florida** | Aquaculture Certificate of Registration | $50/year | Approved species list; non-native species require Special Activity License | FL DACS (Div. of Aquaculture) | Strong aquaculture framework. Must meet Best Management Practices (BMPs). FL is favorable for shrimp farming. Saltwater species in open systems face stricter review |
| **Texas** | Exotic Species Permit; Fish Farm License | $25-$200/year | Non-native species (including *L. vannamei*) require Exotic Species Permit | TX Parks & Wildlife Dept. | *L. vannamei* is considered exotic. Indoor/closed systems face fewer restrictions. Must prevent escapement. Active shrimp farming industry in South TX |
| **California** | Aquaculture Registration | $108-$500+/year | Very restrictive on non-native species. May require Environmental Impact Review | CA Dept. of Fish & Wildlife | One of the most regulated states. Non-native marine shrimp in outdoor ponds are difficult to permit. Indoor RAS systems are more feasible. Coastal Development Permits may also apply |
| **Hawaii** | Aquaculture Lease or Permit | Varies | Relatively permissive for *L. vannamei* (established industry) | HI DLNR; HI Dept. of Agriculture | Long history of shrimp aquaculture. Biosecurity protocols for imports are strict (prevent introduction of disease to island populations). SPF stock strongly preferred |
| **Alabama** | Aquaculture Permit | $25/year | Approved species list; *L. vannamei* generally allowed in closed systems | AL Dept. of Conservation & Natural Resources | Friendly regulatory environment. Auburn University provides strong extension support |
| **Mississippi** | Aquaculture License | $25/year | Check approved species list | MS Dept. of Wildlife, Fisheries & Parks | Generally supportive. Thad Cochran National Warmwater Aquaculture Center is a resource |
| **Louisiana** | Aquaculture License | $25/year | *L. vannamei* allowed with conditions; native species preferred | LA Dept. of Wildlife & Fisheries | Strong aquaculture tradition. Must prevent release of non-native species. Some parishes have additional requirements |
| **Arizona** | Aquaculture Facility License | $25-$100/year | Restricted live wildlife list applies; check approved species | AZ Game & Fish Dept. | Desert conditions make indoor/greenhouse operations attractive. Water rights and usage permits are a significant additional consideration |
| **Southeast (general: SC, NC, GA)** | Varies; typically an aquaculture permit | $10-$100/year | Most allow *L. vannamei* in contained systems | State Dept. of Natural Resources or Agriculture | Generally favorable. SC and NC have growing small-scale shrimp farming communities |
| **Midwest / Northeast (general)** | Aquaculture permit or license; varies widely | $25-$250/year | Non-native marine species may require additional review | State Dept. of Agriculture or Natural Resources | Indoor biofloc and RAS systems avoid many outdoor-species restrictions. Heating costs are the practical barrier, not regulation |

### Tips for Any State

1. **Start with your state's Department of Agriculture** -- most have an aquaculture coordinator.
2. **Check the approved/prohibited species list** before purchasing stock.
3. **Closed/indoor systems** almost always face fewer regulatory hurdles than open ponds.
4. **Keep records** of stock purchases, mortality, water quality, and sales from day one.

## Selling Shrimp for Food

### Regulatory Framework

Selling farm-raised shrimp for human consumption places you under food safety regulation at federal, state, and often local levels.

| Requirement | Details |
|---|---|
| **HACCP Plan** | Required by FDA for all seafood processors. Even a small farm that heads, deveins, or freezes shrimp is considered a processor. HACCP training courses (~$300-$500, 2-3 days) are offered through Sea Grant, state extension, and private providers |
| **FDA Facility Registration** | Any facility that manufactures, processes, packs, or holds food for human consumption must register with FDA (no fee, done online via FDA's FURLS system). Renewed every two years |
| **State Food Safety License** | Most states require a separate food handling/processing license or permit. Fees range from $50-$500/year. Inspections are typically annual |
| **Processing Facility** | If you process (head, peel, devein, freeze, or cook) shrimp, you likely need an inspected processing area. Some states allow on-farm processing facilities; others require use of a licensed commercial kitchen |
| **Labeling** | Must include: product name, net weight, ingredient list (if processed), name and address of producer/packer, safe handling instructions, allergen declaration (shellfish), and nutritional info if required by state |
| **Country of Origin Labeling (COOL)** | Required at retail. Farm-raised domestic shrimp should be labeled "Product of USA, Farm-Raised" |
| **Traceability** | Maintain records of harvest date, lot numbers, buyer information. FDA's FSMA traceability rule (effective 2026) includes shrimp on the Food Traceability List |

### Sales Channels

| Channel | Typical Requirements | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Farmers Markets** | State/local farmers market permit; food handler's card; proper cold chain (ice/refrigeration); some states allow whole live or fresh-on-ice sales with fewer processing requirements | Check if your state's cottage food law covers seafood (most do NOT). Many markets require proof of liability insurance ($1-2M policy) |
| **Direct to Consumer (farm gate)** | Varies by state. Some allow direct sale of whole, unprocessed shrimp with minimal licensing. Others require full food establishment permits | Selling live shrimp directly to consumers at the farm is the least regulated path in many states |
| **Restaurants / Retail** | Full HACCP compliance, facility inspection, liability insurance, and often a wholesale food distribution license | Restaurants often prefer consistent supply. Consider whether you can meet volume and schedule requirements |
| **Online / Shipped** | All of the above plus compliance with shipping perishable food regulations; proper insulated packaging; overnight/2-day shipping | High barrier to entry for small producers due to shipping costs and cold chain requirements |

### Inspections

- Federal (FDA) inspections can occur at any registered facility, though small operations are low priority.
- State inspections are more common -- expect at least annual visits once licensed.
- Keep your facility clean, records organized, and HACCP plan current.

## Selling Live Ornamental Shrimp

The ornamental shrimp market (Neocaridina, Caridina, Sulawesi species) operates under a different regulatory framework than food shrimp.

### Key Regulations

| Area | Details |
|---|---|
| **Interstate Shipping** | The Lacey Act prohibits interstate transport of wildlife in violation of state law. You must comply with BOTH the origin state and destination state laws. Some states require an import permit for live aquatic invertebrates |
| **State Permits** | Many states require a "propagation and sale" or "aquaculture" license even for ornamental invertebrates. Some states (e.g., certain New England states) have no specific permit for freshwater invertebrates; others require them |
| **Health Certificates** | Some states require a health certificate from a licensed veterinarian for live aquatic animals shipped into the state. This is more common for fish but can apply to invertebrates. Check the destination state's requirements |
| **USPS / Carrier Rules** | USPS allows shipping live animals that are not dangerous, poisonous, or hazardous. UPS and FedEx have their own policies. All require proper packaging and labeling ("Live Animals") |
| **Restricted Species** | Some states restrict or prohibit import of specific freshwater invertebrates. Certain Caridina or Sulawesi species may fall under general "exotic species" restrictions |
| **Sales Tax** | Live animal sales are taxable in most states. Collect and remit sales tax where required. Marketplace facilitator laws may apply to online sales platforms |

### Practical Tips for Ornamental Sellers

1. **Research destination state laws** before shipping. The Association of Fish & Wildlife Agencies maintains a state-by-state regulatory database.
2. **Keep breeding records** and be able to demonstrate your stock was captive-bred.
3. **Use DOA (Dead on Arrival) guarantee policies** standard in the hobby -- they build trust and are an industry norm.
4. **Platforms like AquaBid and r/AquaSwap** have their own rules in addition to legal requirements.
5. **Consider business structure** -- even small sellers should have a business license and separate bank account for tax purposes.

## Water Discharge Rules

Improper discharge is one of the most common regulatory pitfalls for small aquaculture operations.

### Federal (Clean Water Act / NPDES)

| Topic | Details |
|---|---|
| **NPDES Permit** | Required for any "point source" discharge of pollutants into waters of the United States. Applies if your tank/pond effluent reaches a stream, river, lake, wetland, or storm drain system |
| **CAAP Threshold** | EPA defines Concentrated Aquatic Animal Production facilities. Below the threshold, you may still need a general permit depending on state rules |
| **General vs. Individual Permit** | Small operations typically fall under a state-administered general permit (less paperwork). Large operations may need an individual NPDES permit |

### State Discharge Rules

Most states administer their own NPDES-equivalent programs. Common requirements:

| Requirement | Typical Details |
|---|---|
| **Effluent limits** | Total suspended solids (TSS), biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), pH, and sometimes nitrogen/phosphorus limits |
| **Monitoring** | May require periodic water sampling and reporting (quarterly or annually) |
| **Settling basins** | Many states require or recommend settling basins/constructed wetlands before discharge |
| **Zero-discharge systems** | Biofloc, RAS, and aquaponics systems that do not discharge to surface waters generally avoid discharge permits entirely |

### Best Practices for Backyard Operations

1. **Design for zero discharge** when possible. Use water for garden irrigation, evaporation, or recirculating systems.
2. **If you must discharge**, use a settling basin and vegetative buffer strip before water reaches any waterway.
3. **Never discharge water containing chemicals** (antibiotics, pesticides, chlorine) into natural waterways.
4. **Document your water management** plan even if not required -- it demonstrates good faith and helps if questions arise.
5. **Test discharge water** periodically for TSS, ammonia, nitrate, and pH to have records of compliance.

## Zoning and Local Regulations

Zoning is often the most immediate and impactful regulatory barrier for backyard shrimp farming.

### Residential Zoning

| Issue | Details |
|---|---|
| **Agricultural use in residential zones** | Most residential zones (R-1, R-2, etc.) do not explicitly permit aquaculture. However, many jurisdictions allow "accessory agricultural use" or have hobby farming exceptions |
| **Home occupation permits** | If you sell product from your home, you may need a home occupation or home-based business permit. These often limit customer visits, signage, and commercial vehicle traffic |
| **Structures** | Greenhouses, sheds, and outbuildings used for shrimp farming may require building permits. Check setback requirements, height limits, and lot coverage percentages |
| **Utilities** | High water usage or electrical loads may trigger utility company review. Some areas have water use restrictions, especially in drought-prone western states |
| **Right-to-Farm laws** | Some states have right-to-farm statutes that protect agricultural operations (including aquaculture in some definitions) from nuisance complaints. Coverage varies -- check if aquaculture is included in your state's definition |

### HOA Restrictions

| Concern | Details |
|---|---|
| **CC&Rs** | Homeowners Association Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions frequently prohibit or restrict agricultural activities, outdoor tanks, visible equipment, and commercial activity on residential lots |
| **Approval process** | Some HOAs allow exceptions with board approval. Submit a detailed, professional plan showing the operation will be contained, quiet, and visually unobtrusive |
| **Common restrictions** | Outdoor tanks/ponds, commercial signage, delivery truck access, odors, and "unsightly" equipment are commonly restricted |
| **Practical tip** | Indoor operations (garage, basement, dedicated room) are much less likely to trigger HOA issues. If your system is not visible from outside, enforcement is rare |

### Noise and Nuisance

| Source | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **Air pumps and blowers** | Regenerative blowers can produce 60-80 dB. Use vibration-dampening mounts, sound enclosures, and locate equipment away from property lines. Linear air pumps are much quieter (35-50 dB) |
| **Backup generators** | May be restricted by noise ordinances (typically 55-65 dB at property line during daytime, 45-55 dB at night). Use inverter generators or battery backup instead |
| **Odor** | Properly managed biofloc and RAS systems have minimal odor. Neglected systems or dead shrimp can produce complaints. Maintain good husbandry to avoid issues |
| **Light** | Greenhouse lighting for 24/7 operations may violate light pollution ordinances. Use blackout curtains or shields |
| **Local noise ordinances** | Most municipalities have specific decibel limits by time of day and zoning district. Check your local code before installing loud equipment |

### Steps to Ensure Compliance

1. **Visit your local planning/zoning office** and ask whether aquaculture or a home-based agricultural operation is permitted in your zone.
2. **Check for any overlay districts** (environmental, historic, flood zone) that may add restrictions.
3. **Read your HOA CC&Rs thoroughly** if applicable.
4. **Talk to your neighbors** before starting. A proactive conversation prevents complaints later.
5. **Get everything in writing** -- verbal approval from a zoning officer is not reliable. Request written confirmation or a formal zoning determination letter.
6. **Consider starting small and indoor** to minimize regulatory exposure while you learn.
